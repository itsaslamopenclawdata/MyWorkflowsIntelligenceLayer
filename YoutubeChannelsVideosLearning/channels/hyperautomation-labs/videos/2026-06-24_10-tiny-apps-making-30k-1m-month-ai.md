## TL;DR

Hyperautomation Labs profiles 10 real, mostly one-person AI-built apps pulling $30K–$1M+ per month — Fly.Peter (Peter Levels' Cursor-built flight sim, ~$50K/mo), LaunchFast (Hassam Bhatti's Amazon-seller research tool, ~$30K MRR), Cleo (LinkedIn growth, ~$62K MRR in 3 months), YouMax/Rizz (Blake Anderson's selfie scoring + dating replies, claimed ~$500K/mo), Blink (Sabrina Matos' Lovable-built safety app, ~$38K/mo), Formula Bot (David Bressler's plain-English-to-spreadsheet, ~$42K/mo), Rank Pill (Modestas Mitkus' SEO automation, +$23K/mo), Cal AI (Zack Yadegari & Henry Langmack, ~$1.4M/mo gross profit, acquired by MyFitnessPal), Photo AI / Headshot Pro (Peter Levels / Danny Postma, ~$150K and ~$300K/mo), and Base44 (Maor's solo AI-app-builder, ~$189K/mo profit, acquired by Wix for $80M). The build stack is identical every time: describe the idea in Cursor / Claude Code / Lovable, bolt on Stripe, ship in a weekend. The four repeatable money shapes are: (1) the wrapper (one annoying task → one tap), (2) the niche tool (one audience with money → one boring monthly subscription), (3) the selfie loop (photo in → score + upgrade out), and (4) the fun object (one shareable thing → ads). The actual lesson: when code is a weekend, code is not the moat — distribution is. Start with the audience, not the app.

## Key Insights

- **Code is no longer the moat.** AI coding tools (Cursor, Claude Code, Lovable, Bolt, Bubble) have collapsed the build step to hours or a weekend. Every founder profiled either had zero coding background or had never built the specific product before.
- **The four money shapes** repeat across all 10 examples:
  1. **The Wrapper** — point AI at one annoying task, do it in one tap (Cal AI, Formula Bot, Headshot Pro, Photo AI).
  2. **The Niche Tool** — pick one audience with money, build the one boring thing they pay monthly for (LaunchFast for Amazon sellers, Cleo for LinkedIn creators, Rank Pill for SEO agencies).
  3. **The Selfie Loop** — analyze a photo, return a score + improvement, charge for the upgrade (YouMax, Rizz).
  4. **The Fun Object** — one shareable thing, monetize with ads inside it (Fly.Peter's in-game billboards).
- **Distribution beats cleverness.** Peter Levels had hundreds of thousands of followers before typing a line; Blake Anderson won on TikTok virality, not code; Maor won by building the picks-and-shovels that other builders use. The real edge in 2026 is an existing audience or a reliable acquisition channel.
- **Speed to ship is the new superpower.** LaunchFast went from idea to $30K MRR in 48 hours of build time. Blink shipped in 45 days. Cal AI went from concept to a multi-million-dollar MyFitnessPal exit. The pattern: ship ugly, ship fast, iterate in public.
- **"Service arbitrage" is a repeatable wedge.** Several apps (Rank Pill, Photo AI, Headshot Pro) take a service that agencies or professionals charge $300–$1000+ for and deliver ~80% of the value via AI for a fraction of the price. This is the highest-leverage starting point.
- **Numbers caveat.** Most figures are self-reported (Indie Hackers, founder tweets, Lovable blog). Verified-by-press numbers exist for Fly.Peter (404 Media), Cal AI (CNBC reviewed financials), Base44 (TechCrunch acquisition confirmation), Photo AI and Headshot Pro (well-documented). YouMax's $500K/mo was flagged by Fortune as not independently verifiable.
- **The picks-and-shovels play is the ceiling.** Base44 sold to Wix for $80M cash in ~6 months because it sold to the same builder market that produces all the other apps on the list. Once you can build, the most valuable next step is building the tools other builders need.
- **Replace typing with a photo.** Cal AI's entire innovation is collapsing food logging from typed entry into a single camera tap. The general principle: every "I have to type/describe this daily" task is a candidate for vision-first UX.
- **Audience first, app second.** The video's closing homework — and the one actionable insight — is to invert the order most founders use. Pick the audience and the channel first, then build the trivial thing they want.
