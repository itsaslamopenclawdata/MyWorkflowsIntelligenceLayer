- **Use case 1: video editing on 17 takes.** Anthropic engineer used Fable 5 + 11 Labs transcription + FFmpeg + Remotion + the `/goal` workflow orchestrator to edit his own launch video. "I'm processing the recording of a bunch of videos in Fable full recording. The script for them is in Fable full recording Fable 5 script. "Something that would have cost hundreds of thousands of dollars to make and using Fable with two video edit is totally possible." (2:22–5:35)
- **Use case 2: Fable 5 as a content engine.** "Fable is not actually that good at writing. It's not good at writing because you haven't put the right inputs, the right strategy, the right weak the engine and then connect it to Fable." Inputs: origin story, "known for," offer, ICP, frameworks, pillars, funnel structures, tone. Put them in a "content brain" (e.g. Obsidian vault). Weekly loop: research → niche scan → topic discovery → 3 hypothesis tests → assets. Fable 5 reads charts via browser. "Fable 5 runs every system." (5:50–7:15)
- **"Low effort is the alpha."** Morgan Linton's first-day takeaway. Fable 5 is going API-priced (not included in Max accounts) around June 22. "You're going to want to obviously not spend a ton of tokens." Use **factory.ai Droid** to orchestrate when to use Fable 5 vs. Opus vs. cheaper models. "Fable low is better than Opus high." (7:30–8:40)
- **Use case 3: landing page copywriting tournament.** The high-leverage move for any SaaS site. "Write my landing page eight different ways. Different hook, different structure each time. Then create five judges, a skeptical CFO, a distracted founder scrolling at midnight, a competitor, my ideal customer, and a conversion copywriter. Have every judge score every version and explain the scores. Kill the losers, merge what worked into a final version, and show me the scoreboard so I can see why it won." Greg's worked example: backyard-ADU tool from ideabrowser.com produced 8 versions × 5 judges × scores-with-explanation → 1 winner. Sample CFO judge line on a "$2,000/month" claim: "that's a claim the page must immediately defend or it reads like a Zillow estimate." Sample midnight-founder judge line on "$100,000 on a guess": "is a gut punch and it stops the scroll." The secret sauce: pitting variants against each other beats a single one-shot by a wide margin. (8:43–13:15)
- **Use case 4: the interview before the build — pushes back on vague answers.** "I want you to build this rough idea. Don't build anything yet. Interview me like Mark Zuckerberg or Sam Altman or Brian Chesky, experts at building companies that get PMF. One question at a time, 15 questions max, hunting for the thing I haven't thought about. Push back when my answers are vague. After the interview, write the full spec and list three ways this fails, then build the V1." Worked example: a habit-widget idea → 15 questions → spec that pivoted it to a "phone number that sees your calendar and texts you with receipts and has a consequence you signed up for." Fable 5's tone matches the prompt persona: when Greg said "I let life get in the way," Fable responded "That's a horoscope answer, Greg. Life got in the way is what everyone says when they haven't watched the tape. You never let a podcast guest get away with that, and you'd say, 'Give me the seed.'" - **Use case 5: hire it to kill your company.** "Here's my P&L, here's my pricing page, my churn data, my last 50 support tickets. You are a well-funded founder who hates me. Spend the day building the company that kills mine. Real plan, positioning, pricing, first 10 customers you'd steal, and the exact email you'd send each one. Then rank every attack by how much of it you can do yourself with no employees. Don't be polite. I'd rather hear it from you than read it in my mind in the churn dashboard." **The "rank by what can execute itself" is the only threat ranking that matters now.** (18:20–19:45)
- **Use case 6: your 1-page operating manual from 2 years of decisions.** "I give it 2 years of your notes, your decision docs, your postmortems. You ask it to map every meaningful decision. What I chose, what I believed at the time, what actually happened. Find my patterns. Where am I systematically too early, too late, too optimistic? What do I always say right before a bad call? Write me the one-page operating manual for working Greg that a COO would secretly keep." Works because Fable 5 has a 1M token context window. (20:00–21:20)
- **Use case 7: what's missing in your business — gap analysis.** "Here's my analytics, my content calendar, and my offer. Don't tell me what's wrong with what exists. Spend your time on what's absent. What is every successful company in my space doing that appears nowhere in my business? What customer am I not even trying to reach? What revenue line should exist and doesn't? Rank by money left on the table." (21:20–22:10)
- **Use case 8: negotiation roleplay.** "I'm negotiating a deal with this counterparty. Here's what I know about them and what I want. Become them. Their incentives, their alternatives, their pressure. We negotiate in rounds. You respond the way they actually would, including going quiet or getting aggressive. After each round, break character and tell me what I just gave away for free. Don't let me win because I'm the user." (22:10–23:07)
- **Use case 9: 80-page contract second opinion.** "Here's the full contract, every exhibit included. Read all of it, especially the tables and schedules. Three lists. Then tell me the three changes you'd request and exactly how to phrase the ask. Flag anything when I should pay where I should pay a real lawyer to look." Greg's caveat: he's not telling you to skip lawyers, he's telling you to **always** run contracts through Fable 5 first, even when working with a real lawyer. Aaron Levie (Box) cited as seeing "incredible outputs." (23:07–24:55)

- **Startup idea 1: synthetic focus group firm.** D2C brands spend ~$50K on media before learning an ad doesn't work. You run the **copy tournament as a business**: 50 ad variants judged overnight by panels built from the brand's actual customer reviews. Charge ~$3,000/launch, costs you ~$100 in tokens. The brand's media buyer becomes your repeat customer. Only possible now because sub-agents can hold distinct personas and argue instead of a model agreeing with itself. Worked prompt: "Here are 500 customer reviews for Athletic Greens and their 10 best ads. Build six customer personas from actual review language, not demographics. Then, take these 15 new ad concepts and have every persona score every ad. Would they stop scrolling? Would they click? What's their objection?
