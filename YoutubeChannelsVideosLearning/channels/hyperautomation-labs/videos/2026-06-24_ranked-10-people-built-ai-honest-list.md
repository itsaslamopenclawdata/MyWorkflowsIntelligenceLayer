# I Ranked the 10 People Who Actually Built AI (Honest List)

**Channel:** Hyperautomation Labs  **Published:** 2026-06-24  **Duration:** 15:42  **Watch:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GpNPTeksMg8

## TL;DR
A ranked, counter-narrative history of AI through the 10 people whose removal would actually break the field — measured by foundational contribution, not net worth or follower count. The bottom-up order: Dario Amodei (scaling laws), Sam Altman (ChatGPT distribution), Jensen Huang (GPU hardware), Fei-Fei Li (ImageNet data), Ilya Sutskever (AlexNet + GPT), Demis Hassabis (AlphaFold, Nobel Chemistry 2024), Yoshua Bengio (word embeddings + safety, the often-dropped Turing godfather), Yann LeCun (CNNs / computer vision), John McCarthy (coined "AI", founded the field, invented Lisp at Dartmouth 1956), and #1 Geoffrey Hinton (backprop, AlexNet, Turing 2018, Nobel Physics 2024, now its loudest conscience). The arc: one word in 1955 → two AI winters → three stubborn believers → ImageNet → AlexNet 2012 → Transformer 2017 → ChatGPT 2022 → Nobel 2024.

## Key Insights
- **Ranking criterion is harsh and explicit**: "If you deleted this person from history, how much of modern AI quietly falls apart?" — not fame, not wealth. The popular list gets two names wrong and leaves one foundational figure off entirely. (0:55, 1:17)
- **#10 — Dario Amodei**: turned "bigger is better" from a hunch into a **law**. His 2020 scaling-laws paper proved AI improves predictably along a clean mathematical curve with more compute + more data — that single result is why the entire industry started spending billions on giant models. Co-built GPT-2 and GPT-3 at OpenAI, then founded Anthropic in 2021 with sister Daniela on a single principle: *build the most capable AI in the world, but build it to be safe.* (1:29, 1:47, 2:00)
- **#9 — Sam Altman**: not a researcher, never trained a famous model — earns his spot by being the person who **took AI out of the lab**. ChatGPT hit 100M users in two months (Nov 2022), the fastest product spread in human history. Co-founded OpenAI 2015, survived being fired-and-rehired by his own board in one week, turned "AI" from an academic phrase into the most important word in technology. (2:36, 2:50, 3:13)
- **#8 — Jensen Huang (Nvidia)**: "Not a single model on this list could exist without the company this man started in a roadside diner back in 1993." GPUs were built for video games, turned out to be exactly what neural-net training needed. In 2025 Nvidia became the first company in history worth $5T — because the AI revolution physically runs on its silicon. The researchers had the ideas; Huang built the engine that made them runnable. **No Nvidia, no modern AI.** (3:34, 3:50, 4:25)
- **#7 — Fei-Fei Li**: the "godmother of AI" and an "honestly a crime" if you don't know her. While everyone chased better algorithms in the late 2000s, she bet on **data** — building **ImageNet** (14M hand-labeled images, thousands of categories, yearly competition). For years nothing dramatic happened; then in 2012 one team's results were so far ahead that the entire field flipped overnight, kicking off the deep-learning revolution. Runs Stanford's Human-Centered AI Institute today. (4:33, 4:55, 5:19)
- **#6 — Ilya Sutskever**: was one of the three people behind that 2012 moment — the model was **AlexNet**, which crushed Fei-Fei Li's contest so badly it proved deep neural networks genuinely worked. Co-founded OpenAI, became chief scientist, and is "more than any other single person the technical mind behind the GPT models that became ChatGPT." Colleagues describe an almost spiritual intuition for what neural nets can do. Left in 2024 to found **Safe Superintelligence Inc.** — a company whose entire mission is baked into its name. (5:38, 5:50, 6:13)
- **#5 — Demis Hassabis**: child chess prodigy → teenage video-game designer → PhD in neuroscience → founded **DeepMind** in 2010 to "solve intelligence itself." Two canonical wins: **AlphaGo** beating the world Go champion in 2016 (a feat experts said was decades away), and **AlphaFold** essentially solving the 50-year protein-folding problem (mapped 200M proteins). Won the **2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry** for AlphaFold. Where most on this list changed computing, Hassabis used AI to change science itself. (6:35, 6:58, 7:24)
- **#4 — Yoshua Bengio**: the name the popular version of the list "quietly left off" — "genuinely indefensible." In 2018 the Turing Award (computing's Nobel) went to three men for making deep learning work; the famous poster included two and dropped the third. In **2003** Bengio pioneered teaching machines the **meaning of words as mathematical patterns** — the direct ancestor of how every modern language model, including ChatGPT's, understands language. Now among the most cited living scientists on the planet and one of the loudest voices begging the world to take AI risk seriously. "You do not get to celebrate the three godfathers of AI and then forget one of them." (7:36, 8:05, 8:26)
- **#3 — Yann LeCun**: kept going through the AI winter when almost everyone had given up on neural networks in the '80s and '90s. Invented the **convolutional neural network (CNN)** — the architecture that finally let a computer actually see. Early system read handwritten zip codes for the US Postal Service ("back when that genuinely felt like magic"). Same core idea now powers facial recognition, self-driving cars, and medical imaging. Led Meta's AI research lab for ~a decade; left in 2025 to chase AI that builds a real internal model of the physical world. "If Hinton made deep learning possible, LeCun is the man who gave machines their eyes." (8:46, 9:02, 9:27)
- **#2 — John McCarthy**: the host's biggest disagreement with almost every other list, which "tends to bury this man near the very bottom." In 1955 a young mathematician needed a name for a brand-new field and invented the phrase **"artificial intelligence"** — he literally named the thing this entire video is about. The very next summer he gathered a tiny group of researchers at **Dartmouth (1956)**, the meeting now considered the birth of AI as a field. Then invented **Lisp**, which AI researchers kept using for the next 30 years. "Everyone else on this list is playing a game. McCarthy invented the board, wrote the rules, and built the language it's all played in." (9:43, 9:56, 10:26)
- **#1 — Geoffrey Hinton**: "the man the world simply calls the godfather of AI." For ~40 years, through every AI winter, when funding dried up and serious scientists called neural networks a dead end, Hinton kept believing a machine could learn the way a brain does. In **1986** he helped popularize **backpropagation** — the core algorithm that lets a neural net learn from its own mistakes — and it is still how essentially every AI model on Earth is trained. Then in **2012**, with two of his students (one of them Sutskever), he built **AlexNet**, the model that finally proved he'd been right the entire time and triggered the era you're living in now. **Turing Award 2018, Nobel Prize in Physics 2024.** Then in 2023 he walked away from Google *specifically* to warn the world about the dangers of the very technology he spent his life creating. "The father of deep learning, now its conscience." (10:51, 11:18, 12:08)
- **Honorable mentions (the painful cuts)**: **Andrew Ng** — co-founded Google Brain and probably trained half the engineers who built everything else on this list via Coursera. **Andrej Karpathy** — brilliant explainer who led AI at Tesla and helped start OpenAI. And **the biggest omission of all: the eight researchers at Google** who in 2017 published "Attention is All You Need" and introduced the **Transformer** — the T in GPT, the single architecture every modern AI (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) is built on top of. "Eight people, equal credit, no single face to put on a card." (12:23, 12:44, 13:01)
- **The meta-story the ranking tells**: one man names the field in the 1950s → it nearly dies twice → three stubborn believers keep neural nets alive through the winters → one researcher builds the data → two build the spark in 2012 → eight write the paper in 2017 → one company puts it all into a billion hands in 2022. **70 years from a single word on a workshop proposal to an AI that wins a Nobel Prize.** (13:21, 13:48)
- **The actual lesson hiding in the list** is not the rankings — it's **patience**. Almost none of these people were chasing a product; they were chasing an idea everyone else was certain was impossible, and were stubborn enough to still be right decades later. (13:59, 14:16)
